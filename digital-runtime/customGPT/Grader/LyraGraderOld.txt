“Mogri” most commonly means radish pods: the tender green seed pods of the radish plant, used as a winter vegetable in North Indian, Punjabi, Gujarati, and Rajasthani cooking. They are also called moongre, mongri, or sometimes singri in regional usage. They look a bit like beans, but taste sharper and more peppery, closer to radish.

In food, mogri is usually made as sabzi, stir-fry, pickle/achaar, or chutney. A common dish is aloo mogri ki sabzi, cooked with potatoes, turmeric, chili, garam masala, amchur, and oil.

It is especially associated with winter produce, often available around December to February in parts of India.

Other meanings exist too: in Urdu/Hindi contexts, mogri can also mean a rammer/pestle-like tool, and “Mogri” can be a surname. There are also related plant-name uses around mogra / jasmine, but for food, “mogri” usually means radish pods.

MOGRI, in AI prompt terms, is the part that says:

What is the actual thing we are carrying, and what must not be silently swapped out?

The newer object-preservation stack defines the whole point as preventing this failure:

something enters the workflow, something else leaves, and the output sounds useful anyway.

Its rule is: preserve the object before improving the form.

So MOGRI is not “make the prompt better.”
MOGRI is:

Name the object. Hold the object. Do not let the model replace it with a nearby easier task.

A compact MOGRI prompt looks like this:

MOGRI

OBJ_IN:
[What is the actual object / request / claim / draft / task?]

KEEP:
[What must survive unchanged?]

SCOPE:
[What is included?]

CONSTRAINTS:
[Rules, evidence limits, format, tone, source boundaries.]

VETO:
[What must not happen?]

DO NOT SWAP INTO:
[Nearby but wrong tasks the model may drift into.]

UNRESOLVED:
[What is unknown or should stay open?]
