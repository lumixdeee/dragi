Mogri usually refers to radish pods—the long, green or purple seed pods of the radish plant. They’re also called moongra, moongre, mungre, singri, or mooli ki phali in different parts of India. In Indian cooking, especially North and West Indian food, mogri is treated as a winter vegetable.

It has a crisp texture and a peppery radish-like taste, but usually milder than the radish root. Shorter pods, around 2–5 inches, are often considered tastier and less fibrous than very long ones.

Common ways to use mogri include mogri ki sabzi with potatoes and spices, kachumber/salad, chutney, pickle/achaar, stir-fries, or adding it to kadhi and mixed vegetables. A typical sabzi uses mogri with potato, turmeric, chili powder, amchur, garam masala, and mustard oil or another cooking oil.

It’s especially valued in Jain cooking because it gives a radish-like flavor without using the radish root, which many Jains avoid. It’s also considered nutritious, with vitamins, minerals, antioxidants, and the general benefits associated with cruciferous vegetables.

When buying mogri, look for pods that are fresh, firm, bright, and not overly thick or stringy. To prepare it, wash well, trim the ends, chop into small pieces, and cook briefly if you want to keep some crunch.

Yep — Dragi looks like the companion idea to Mogri.

In the creator/community wording, Dragi is also called “dragonruntime” and is described as a way to define a “beast” by asking four questions: what does it eat, where does it live, how will you call it, and what eats it. The GitHub repo also lists the “four foes” as beast, best, post, pest, and the “four controls” as war, law, wall, roar.

The compressed prompt version I found is:

DRAGI=Qs(Eat;Loc;ID;Eater)Foes(Beast,best,post,pest)Conts(law,roar,wall,war)Fixed. No redefinition. R=VAR.

A Reddit post from the same ecosystem says: “Mogri holds the object, Dragi defines the object.”

Plain English:

Dragi = define the thing clearly enough that the AI knows what it is, what feeds it, where it operates, what can break it, and what rules contain it.

My promptbot translation:

Use DRAGI to define the object before solving.

Object:
- ID: What is this thing called?
- Eat: What inputs, resources, data, or assumptions does it consume?
- Loc: Where does it operate? What context, domain, or environment contains it?
- Eater: What uses it, constrains it, threatens it, or can invalidate it?

Foes:
- Beast: the raw uncontrolled version of the thing
- Best: over-optimization or chasing an abstract ideal
- Post: after-the-fact narrative drift
- Pest: noise, edge cases, distractions, or adversarial interference

Controls:
- Law: fixed rules and invariants
- Wall: boundaries and exclusions
- Roar: signals, outputs, or visible behavior
- War: conflict handling and tradeoffs

Do not redefine the object mid-answer. Keep fixed definitions stable. Treat R as variable unless explicitly fixed.

DRAGI = define the object.
