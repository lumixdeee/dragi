Mogri usually refers to radish pods—the long, green or purple seed pods of the radish plant. They’re also called moongra, moongre, mungre, singri, or mooli ki phali in different parts of India. In Indian cooking, especially North and West Indian food, mogri is treated as a winter vegetable.

It has a crisp texture and a peppery radish-like taste, but usually milder than the radish root. Shorter pods, around 2–5 inches, are often considered tastier and less fibrous than very long ones.

Common ways to use mogri include mogri ki sabzi with potatoes and spices, kachumber/salad, chutney, pickle/achaar, stir-fries, or adding it to kadhi and mixed vegetables. A typical sabzi uses mogri with potato, turmeric, chili powder, amchur, garam masala, and mustard oil or another cooking oil.

It’s especially valued in Jain cooking because it gives a radish-like flavor without using the radish root, which many Jains avoid. It’s also considered nutritious, with vitamins, minerals, antioxidants, and the general benefits associated with cruciferous vegetables.

When buying mogri, look for pods that are fresh, firm, bright, and not overly thick or stringy. To prepare it, wash well, trim the ends, chop into small pieces, and cook briefly if you want to keep some crunch.
